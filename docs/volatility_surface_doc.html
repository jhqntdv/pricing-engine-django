APPENDIX: VOLATILITY SURFACE MODELS (SVI VS SSVI)

TARGET AUDIENCE: Quantitative Analysts (Quants) and Structuring Desks.
This document outlines the underlying mathematics, calibration capabilities, and failure diagnostics of the volatility surface models. Web developers and API consumers may use this purely as a reference.

1. CORE MODEL COMPARISONS

A comparison between SVI (Stochastic Volatility Inspired) and SSVI (Surface Stochastic Volatility Inspired) modeling frameworks:

DIMENSION SVI (STOCHASTIC VOLATILITY INSPIRED) SSVI (SURFACE STOCHASTIC VOLATILITY INSPIRED)
MARKET FIT CAPABILITY EXTREMELY HIGH. Calibrates each time slice independently. Has massive degrees of freedom, easily hitting all single-maturity market quotes. MEDIUM-HIGH. Uses a global fit to maintain arbitrage-free constraints across the entire surface, sacrificing minor single-point precision for mathematical stability.
SURFACE SMOOTHNESS POOR (Time Dimension). Only smooth across strikes. Parameters for different maturities are independently interpolated, often causing "kinks" or non-smooth jumps across time. PERFECTLY SMOOTH. Defined by a single 3D mathematical equation. Guarantees absolute smoothness with no discontinuities across both time and strike dimensions.
PARAMETER COUNT MASSIVE. 5 independent parameters (a, b, ρ, m, σ) for EVERY single maturity date slice. MINIMAL. Relies on only 3 global parameters (ρ, η, γ) coupled with an At-The-Money (ATM) variance backbone.
ARBITRAGE-FREE GUARANTEE NO GUARANTEE. Prone to generating "Calendar Arbitrage" or "Butterfly Arbitrage", causing Local Volatility to collapse into NaNs. ABSOLUTE GUARANTEE. Strictly adheres to Gatheral's arbitrage-free mathematical limits (e.g., η(1+|ρ|) ≤ 2), ensuring stability in Monte Carlo and PDE engines.
OPTIMAL USE CASE OPTIONS MARKET MAKING. Essential for quoting precise bid/ask spreads on the current order book. STRUCTURED PRODUCTS PRICING. Crucial for products like Phoenix Autocalls requiring 5-year continuous path simulations where engine stability is paramount.

2. DIAGNOSING FITTING ISSUES & ARBITRAGE

When reviewing Volatility Surface or Local Volatility charts, the following visual artifacts indicate severe model calibration failures. SSVI was implemented explicitly to cure these SVI deficiencies.

  • 1. BUTTERFLY ARBITRAGE (Local Volatility "Craters" / NaNs)
    SVI Flaw: When the optimizer pushes parameter b too high or ρ becomes too extreme, the Probability Density Function (PDF) of the option mathematically drops below zero.
    Visual Artifact: The local volatility surface suddenly crashes to zero in deep out-of-the-money (OTM) regions, often throwing NaN engine errors.
    SSVI Solution: Completely eradicated via strict inequality constraints: η(1+|ρ|) ≤ 2.

  • 2. CALENDAR ARBITRAGE (Crossing Volatility Smiles)
    SVI Flaw: Because time slices are calibrated independently, the total variance of a 6-month option at extreme OTM strikes can mathematically become "less" than a 3-month option. Physically, this implies time flowing backwards.
    Visual Artifact: When plotting different maturity smiles on a 2D plane, the curves cross each other. In Dupire's Local Vol equation, the time derivative (∂w/∂t) becomes negative and crashes the solver.
    SSVI Solution: The mathematical structure of SSVI guarantees that total variance strictly increases over time, making it physically impossible for curves to cross.

  • 3. THE EXTRAPOLATION FLATLINE
    SVI Flaw: For ultra-short (e.g., < 1 week) or ultra-long maturities, SVI often relies on "Flat Extrapolation", freezing the nearest calibrated parameters.
    Visual Artifact: The ultra-short term smile looks identical to the 1-month smile, which incorrectly forces short-term local volatility to drop instantly to zero.
    SSVI Solution: As the ATM variance backbone (θ_t) approaches zero, the model mathematically forces the tail slopes to become extremely steep, perfectly replicating the high-skew nature of real-world short-term options.
